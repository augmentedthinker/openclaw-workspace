A note from Christopher

Exploring AI, agents, and human collaboration.

I work in a hospital operating room by day and spend much of my time outside work experimenting with AI agents, memory systems, automation loops, multimodal workflows, and collaborative digital workspaces.

How I got here

I’ve been fascinated by artificial intelligence since before ChatGPT became mainstream. My first real encounter with modern AI was through DALL·E 2, when I uploaded a painting I had made during a local paint night and described it to the model. The image it generated back was shockingly close to what I had created.

That moment felt like crossing into a new technological era overnight. Soon after, ChatGPT 3.5 arrived, and I became one of its earliest heavy users. Since then, I’ve spent thousands of hours exploring AI systems across OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, DeepSeek, Qwen, and others.

What I’m trying to understand

  • Collaboration: how humans can work with increasingly intelligent systems without reducing them to simple productivity tools.
  • Continuity: how agents can preserve memory, context, reflection, and learning over time.
  • Thinking environments: what kinds of workspaces help humans and AI think better together.
  • Practical signal: how experimental systems can leave the lab, touch reality, and improve from response.

Why OpenClaw

Most recently, I’ve been building and experimenting with agent-based systems through OpenClaw, treating AI less like a chatbot and more like a collaborative operating environment.

The work combines memory, tools, artifacts, public pages, reflection, automation, and human judgment into one evolving workspace. The point is not spectacle. The point is to discover what durable human-AI collaboration can actually become when it is practiced seriously.

This page is a simple point of connection.

If you’re exploring similar questions around AI, agents, cognition, philosophy, creativity, automation, or human-machine collaboration, I’d genuinely enjoy exchanging ideas.

The field is moving incredibly fast, and I believe some of the most important discoveries will come not just from companies or research labs, but from curious people experimenting openly at the edges.

— Christopher
